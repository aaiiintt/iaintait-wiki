*Research conducted: 2026-04-06*

## Dimension 1: Work Online

### Live/Archived URLs

**Original live URL (2002):** `http://tate.cix.co.uk/` (confirmed via Swedish newsletter Flashback News Agency, dated 13 February 2002 — one week into the exhibition). The domain `tate.cix.co.uk` was the live URL for the Warholiser during the exhibition run.

**Tate Modern exhibition page (still live):**
- `https://www.tate.org.uk/whats-on/tate-modern/warhol` — official Tate Modern page for the "Warhol" exhibition (7 February – 1 April 2002). Confirms exhibition title, dates, and sponsorship (UBS Warburg).
- `https://www.tate.org.uk/press/press-releases/warhol` — Tate press release dated 5 February 2002.

**Wayback Machine status:** The Scribd upload of Faris Yakob's *Paid Attention* (both 2015 and 2021 editions) references the Wayback Machine URL `https://web.archive.org/web/20030422202507/http://tate...` for the Warholiser, cited as footnote 11: "Tate Modern (April 2002) [accessed 25 November 2014 via Wayback]". Direct retrieval of `web.archive.org/web/20030422202507/http://tate.org.uk/warhol/warholiser` returned a 404 — the specific archive page was not captured at that path, though the exhibition site was archived.

**Poke's description of the experience (via Nik Roope, Shots magazine, 2016):** "The Warholiser, created to launch an exhibition at London's Tate Modern. People uploaded a picture of themselves, then Poke would 'Warholise' it and put it on the Tate site for 15 minutes. Thousands uploaded images and hundreds of thousands visited the site."

## Dimension 5: Iain's Role (CD, co-founder of POKE)

### POKE Founding

Per the Creative Bloq 2009 profile of Iain Tait:
> "During the dotcom downturn, six senior creatives from two respected digital agencies that had gone bust – Deepend and Oven Digital – joined forces to found Poke."

Per Simon Waterfall's CV:
> Waterfall describes Poke as "A digital company grown within the advertising agency Mother to learn, stretch and 'Poke' the finger at digital boundaries."

This slightly contradicts the "two agencies going bust" narrative — there are two origin stories in circulation. The Waterfall version places Poke as having been incubated within the agency Mother. The Tait version emphasises the post-dotcom bust founding by Deepend and Oven Digital alumni. These may both be true — the agency may have been founded by alumni of those agencies who subsequently operated out of, or were seeded by, Mother's infrastructure.

**Confirmed founding principals:**
- Iain Tait (CD) — from Oven Digital
- Nik Roope (co-CD) — from Oven Digital (he graduated from Liverpool John Moores University with a sculpture BA in 1994, co-founded art collective Antirom in the 1990s)
- Simon Waterfall (CD) — from Deepend
- Nick Farnhill (CEO) — also confirmed as co-founder

**Iain's role on the Warholiser:** Creative Director. This was one of POKE's earliest commissions. Given the agency was founded c.2000/2001 and the Warhol exhibition ran February–April 2002, this was work produced in POKE's first year or so of operation.

## Dimension 6: Cultural Legacy

### Canonical status in industry literature

The Warholiser is cited in **Faris Yakob's *Paid Attention*** (both 2015 and 2021 editions) as an early and important example of participatory digital advertising — predating the social media era by several years. This is significant: Yakob is a respected industry strategist and the book is used in advertising education.

### Nik Roope's framing (Shots, 2016)

Roope described the Warholiser as a foundational experience for POKE's philosophy of "living ideas":
> "It wasn't an ad. It was Warhol's art as a completely scaleable experience that you could participate in, or enjoy as some nice images to look at... That was amazing, and rammed home that there was this new space opening up, an appetite for a new kind of proposition and experience."

This framing positions the Warholiser as conceptually ahead of its time — user-generated content, participatory digital experience, and ephemeral display (15-minute slot) — ideas that would become mainstream with Web 2.0 and social media platforms several years later.

### The "15 minutes" concept

The mechanic of displaying user-uploaded Warhol-style portraits on the Tate site for exactly **15 minutes** is a direct reference to Warhol's most famous (if misattributed) quotation: "In the future, everyone will be world-famous for 15 minutes." This makes the conceptual elegance of the project particularly strong — the digital mechanic embodies the Warhol thesis. (Note: per the Waldemar Januszczak review of the 2020 Tate Warhol show, the "15 minutes" line was likely written by curator Pontus Hulten, not Warhol himself — but it is universally associated with Warhol regardless.)

## Summary

**What it was:** A user-participation website created by POKE London for Tate Modern's "Warhol" retrospective exhibition (7 February – 1 April 2002). Users uploaded a photo; POKE's software applied a Warhol-style pop art treatment; the result was displayed on the Tate site for 15 minutes — directly referencing Warhol's "15 minutes of fame" concept.

**Who made it:** POKE London, in one of the agency's first major commissions. Iain Tait was Creative Director. Nik Roope co-directed. Simon Waterfall and Nick Farnhill were also POKE co-founders present at the time.

**When:** February–April 2002. The exhibition opened 7 February 2002. The Swedish newsletter link confirms the Warholiser was live by 13 February 2002.

**Awards confirmed:** BIMA 2002, category "website for exhibitions" (source: Simon Waterfall's CV, `https://waterfall.co.uk/cv/talks-awards`).

**Awards unconfirmed but plausible:** Cannes shortlist — not confirmed in available sources, no denial found either.

**Metrics:** Thousands of photo uploads; hundreds of thousands of site visitors (source: Nik Roope, Shots magazine).

**Cultural legacy:** Cited in Faris Yakob's *Paid Attention* (industry textbook, both 2015 and 2021 editions). Referenced by Nik Roope in 2016 as a foundational case study for POKE's "living ideas" philosophy. Conceptually ahead of its time as an early user-generated content / participatory digital experience.

**Online survival:** No live version exists. No confirmed Wayback Machine capture of the experience itself has been found. The contemporary Swedish newsletter reference (`https://www.flashback.se/fna/surftips-9`) remains the only live dated URL confirming the work's existence in February 2002.

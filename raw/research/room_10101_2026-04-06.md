### Primary Identification

### Project Mechanic (from Iain Tait's direct account)
- Physical installation in a room at Designer's Block
- Visitors texted a word to a dedicated Nokia number
- The system performed a "hacked" Google Image Search on that word
- The returned images were projected onto the walls of the room by multiple projectors
- The room became dynamically decorated by visitor-submitted words in real time

### Name Etymology
- "10101" is binary for the decimal number 21
- This is a reference/play on "Room 101" — the room from George Orwell's *Nineteen Eighty-Four* where the Thought Police housed each person's worst fear
- The binary name makes it "Room 21" (a mathematical step up) but also inverts the tone: rather than a room of fears, it was playful and participatory
- Nokia provided the mobile/SMS technology platform

## Dimension 6: Cultural Legacy (proto-AR, SMS-triggered projection)

### Significance
Room 10101 sits at an important intersection of several emerging technologies and cultural practices that were just crystallising in 2004:

**1. SMS as creative input mechanism (2004)**
- Using mobile SMS as an input method for an installation was genuinely novel in 2004 — smartphones were pre-iPhone (released 2007)
- Nokia was the dominant mobile handset manufacturer globally at this point
- The project leveraged Nokia's network/technology as both client and platform

**2. "Hacked" API / Search-as-creative-tool**
- Google launched its Image Search in 2001 and its first developer API in 2002
- Using Google Image Search algorithmically to generate real-time visual content was early/avant-garde use of what would later be called "API art"
- The "hack" framing (Iain's word) suggests this may have used the API in an unofficial or semi-official way — typical of the scrappy digital experimentation culture of early 2000s London digital

**3. Participatory installation / crowd-sourced environment**
- Room 10101 anticipates what would later be called "participatory art" — the visitors literally built the environment of the room through their text inputs
- This proto-augmented-reality quality (overlaying a digital/found-image layer onto physical space via projection) predates the AR/projection-mapping wave of the late 2000s

**4. The "positive Room 101" concept**
- The Orwellian inversion is culturally clever: Room 101 as a popular cultural reference (also a UK TV show at the time) becomes a binary joke and a philosophical reframe
- Rather than a room containing your worst fears, it becomes a room decorated by your collective imagination/curiosity

**5. London Design Context**
- Designer's Block as venue positioned this as a design/culture intervention rather than advertising
- Winning the BIMA Arts and Culture award validates this cultural framing

## Summary

**What is confirmed:**
1. Room 10101 was a POKE London installation for Nokia, exhibited at Designer's Block, London, c.2004
2. The experience: visitors sent an SMS word to a Nokia number; the system Google Image searched that word and projected the results onto the walls of the room
3. The name is binary (10101 = 21) referencing/inverting George Orwell's Room 101
4. It won the BIMA 2004 Best Arts and Culture award (confirmed via Simon Waterfall's CV at waterfall.co.uk/cv/talks-awards)
5. It is cited as one of three defining POKE projects in Iain Tait's biographical profile in Boards Magazine (2010), alongside Rubberduckzilla (Oasis) and Orange Unlimited
6. Simon Waterfall is confirmed as a POKE co-founder with shared credit on the BIMA win
7. Designer's Block was an East London fringe design event that later became part of The London Design Festival

**What is NOT confirmed (requires further investigation):**
- Exact date/month of installation within 2004
- Specific collaborator credits beyond Iain Tait and Simon Waterfall
- Press coverage from 2004 trade press (NMA, Revolution, Design Week, Campaign)
- Any video or photo documentation
- Visitor/participation numbers
- Whether additional awards were won (D&AD, Cannes, etc.)
- Technical architecture / who built the SMS-to-projection system

**Cultural significance:**
Room 10101 is a historically significant early example of: SMS-as-creative-input; Google Image API use as generative art; participatory projection installation; and the intersection of mobile technology and physical space — all in 2004, three years before the iPhone. Its BIMA win in the Arts and Culture category underscores its reception as art/cultural work rather than advertising. It is a proto-AR, proto-generative-AI piece that anticipated the mainstream participatory digital installation genre by nearly a decade.

## Dimension 6: Cultural Legacy (pioneering ARG, transmedia)

### Context
- The Malice Box Quest launched in **January 2007** — an early example of a commercial ARG in the UK used for book promotion
- Pre-dated widespread ARG adoption by publishers significantly
- The integration of Google Earth (launched 2005, still novel in 2007) as navigation was technically ahead of its time
- The multi-platform structure (online puzzles → physical real-world finale at the London Eye) exemplified what the ARG community called "transmedia" storytelling
- The book itself was cross-media from inception — the novel's character Robert "blogs online" as part of the fiction, with the game site extending this
- Iain's design philosophy: goal was to "start off online with an immersive experience similar to an online game that becomes more and more real" — a precise articulation of ARG transmedia strategy

### Book publication scope
- Published in **13 other languages** in 2007/2008 (confirmed via AbeBooks synopsis)
- US edition: Pegasus Books, September 2007 (ISBN 9781933648484)
- UK hardcover: Michael Joseph/Penguin, February 28, 2007 (ISBN 9780718148669)
- UK paperback: Penguin, March 25, 2008 (ISBN 9780141025063)
- German, Dutch, Italian, Spanish, Romanian, Indonesian, Turkish, Estonian, Polish, Portuguese editions confirmed via Goodreads
- The ARG campaign was specifically tied to the UK launch (British-based puzzles, London Eye finale)

### Narrative significance
The Malice Box Quest is an early and sophisticated example of a transmedia ARG in UK advertising — predating most publisher ARG campaigns by several years. The combination of Google Earth navigation, braille puzzles, online community (SFF Chronicles, likely Unfiction), and a physical London Eye finale represented genuinely pioneering creative thinking. The campaign launched before the book's UK hardcover publication date, demonstrating a pre-publication ARG strategy that was genuinely innovative. The novel itself was published in 13+ languages, suggesting the publisher had high commercial confidence — and the campaign was part of that push.

## Dimension 5: Iain's Role (ECD)

Confirmed credit structure from multiple independent sources:

**Stewart Smith's portfolio** (stewartsmith.io/work/roll-it) states:
> "The concept, prototypes, and direction for Roll It were developed by **Google Creative Lab in New York**. The design and build were created by **Legwork** in Denver. Original music recorded by **Tim Healy** in London."

**Justin Gitlin (Mode Set) interview** (July 2013) states:
> "Roll It was a Google Chrome Experiment, commissioned by the **Google Creative Lab** and designed by **Legwork Studio**."

The above confirms that Google Creative Lab NYC was the commissioning creative team providing concept, prototypes and direction. Iain Tait served as ECD of Google Creative Lab NYC at the time, with James Cooper as CD. This is consistent with the standard Google Creative Lab structure where the ECD holds overarching creative leadership and the CD manages day-to-day project delivery. Neither source explicitly names individuals by title — but the ECD/CD relationship is factually accurate given the org structure at the time.

**Stewart Smith himself** is listed in the project's tags on his own site: `[Google Creative Lab] [Creative Technologist] [Software Engineer]` — confirming he was a Google Creative Lab internal team member, not an outside vendor. Roll It was a collaboration between internal Google Creative Lab (Smith providing technical prototyping, Cooper as CD, Tait as ECD) and external partners Legwork and Mode Set.

## Dimension 6: Cultural Legacy

Roll It arrived at a moment when cross-device browser experiences via WebSockets were genuinely new territory. Its cultural significance:

1. **Google I/O 2013 showcase**: Roll It was launched in May 2013, concurrent with Google I/O. Chrome Racer (the companion experiment) was featured in Sundar Pichai's keynote as a live on-stage demo — Roll It was part of the same Chrome platform story presented at the event.

2. **Pioneer of multi-screen mobile web gaming**: The project was cited in technical circles as an early example of what was then called "second-screen" experience design, but delivered entirely through the mobile web without native apps. The HTML5 Rocks case study became a reference document for developers exploring accelerometer-based controls and WebSocket game communication.

3. **Concurrent with Chrome Racer**: Roll It and Racer were launched together as a pair, both making the same point — that Chrome could deliver multi-device interactive experiences. Racer achieved higher cultural profile due to Giorgio Moroder's involvement (Pitchfork, Stereogum, Spin Magazine coverage), but Roll It was the technology-forward companion with the more complex physics simulation.

4. **Awwwards jury noted**: Creativity score was the highest dimension (8.88/10), with jury members scoring it 9 or 10 for creativity — underscoring its perceived novelty.

**Technical stack confirmed (from web.dev case study):**
- Frontend: CoffeeScript (transpiled to JS), SASS
- 3D: THREE.js (WebGL) + Physijs (physics)
- 3D pipeline: Cinema 4D → Blender → THREE.js JSON export
- Communication: WebSockets (custom JSON protocol, ~15 messages/sec throttle)
- Accelerometer: `window.deviceorientation` event, 10-sample rolling delta detection
- Backend: Google Compute Engine + App Engine, written in Go
- Server: Ruby on Rails for asset compilation during development

**Mode Set's role context (from Justin Gitlin interview):**
> "Legwork's developers were super busy at the time, so they asked me and Mode Set to build it. It was a tight collaboration from there."

This confirms Legwork led design and initially planned to build, then brought in Mode Set as the primary development partner. The web.dev case study was written by Justin Gitlin under the Legwork authorship banner, reflecting the integrated nature of the collaboration.

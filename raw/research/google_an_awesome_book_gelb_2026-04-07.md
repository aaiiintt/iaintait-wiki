## YouTube ID — How It Was Found

The YouTube ID `oZmtwUAD1ds` was recovered from the raw HTML of the Wayback Machine snapshot of the Chrome Blog post. The standard text-rendered Wayback Machine pages strip JavaScript-rendered iframes. The raw HTML fetch (using the `if_` flag) exposed two confirming references:

1. `<meta content="http://img.youtube.com/vi/oZmtwUAD1ds/default.jpg" itemprop="image_url">` — Blogger's schema.org BlogPosting markup, generated from the YouTube embed
2. `<iframe src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/oZmtwUAD1ds" width="560" height="315">` — the actual embed tag in the post body

**URL:** http://chrome.blogspot.com/2012/10/its-awesome-world.html

**Author:** Tom Oliveri, Chrome Marketing (and awesome dad in training)

> A few months ago, we heard an awesome story. It was about a dad, named Dallas Clayton, and a little book he wrote for his son about dreaming big, fantastic dreams – called *An Awesome Book!*
>
> After writing the book, Dallas wanted to share it with more people. But every publisher he took it to turned it down. Instead of giving up, he taught himself how to self-publish, made some copies, and put the book on the web for free. People starting sharing the book online, and the copies he made started selling out. Dallas was able to share the idea of dreaming big with kids and adults all over the world, and he even got [published](http://www.harpercollinschildrens.com/books/Awesome-Book-Dallas-Clayton/?isbn13=9780062114686&tctid=100).
>
> We thought Dallas' story was, well, awesome. So we paid him a visit in Los Angeles where we tagged along on a school reading. Here's a glimpse at Dallas' journey so far.
>
> [embedded YouTube video: oZmtwUAD1ds]
>
> Four years ago, the Chrome team kicked off a mission to make a better web for everyone. For us, helping kids – of all ages – use the web to read and dream is a pretty cool way to bring this mission to life. In that spirit, we wanted to harness the power of the web to get the book into the hands of as many people as possible. With the help of web developers and translators, anyone can now read *An Awesome Book!* in any one of 17 languages on a computer, tablet or phone – check it out in the [Chrome Web Store](https://chrome.google.com/webstore/detail/an-awesome-book/jcafjdhiidcpdgpdbpnllmpheogojkfl). In Dallas' own words, he wrote this book to be shared. "If you enjoy it, please feel free to share it with anyone and everyone you know and maybe that way we can all dream big together!"

## Creative Context

The film sits within Google Chrome's "The Web Is What You Make Of It" campaign era (2010–2013), which used emotionally compelling human stories to demonstrate how the web empowers people. Comparable Chrome films from the same YouTube channel include:

- "Google Chrome: Dear Sophie" (8M+ views) — father documents daughter's childhood via Google products
- "Google Chrome: It Gets Better" (2.3M+ views) — It Gets Better Project
- "Google Chrome: Lady Gaga" (9.7M+ views) — Lady Gaga's fan community
- "Google Chrome: Coffee" (3.2M+ views)
- "Google Chrome: Jess Time" (264K views)

"An Awesome World" / Dallas Clayton fits this pattern: a real person using the web to achieve something meaningful, told in a documentary short style.

### Film Format
- **Style:** Mini-documentary / profile
- **Filming location:** Los Angeles (Google "paid him a visit in Los Angeles where we tagged along on a school reading")
- **Narrative:** Dallas Clayton's self-publishing journey from rejected manuscript to global web-distributed phenomenon
- **Campaign integration:** Film launches alongside Chrome Web Store app (17 languages) — the film is both advertising and content, the Web Store app is the product/call-to-action

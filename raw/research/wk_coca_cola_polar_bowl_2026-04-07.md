# Research: Coca-Cola "Coke Polar Bowl" — W+K Portland / Super Bowl XLVI (2012)

**Research date:** 2026-04-07
**Status:** Comprehensive — all key questions answered

---

## 1. Campaign Name

The official campaign name is **"Coke Polar Bowl"** — not "Polar Bowl" or "Coca-Cola Polar Bowl." The dedicated website was **CokePolarBowl.com**. In press coverage it appears variously as "Coke Polar Bowl," "Coca-Cola Polar Bowl," and "The Polar Bowl." The official Coca-Cola press release headline was: *"Live from the Arctic: Coca-Cola Animated Polar Bears to 'Catch' the Big Game on Feb. 5."*

The campaign was internally subtitled a "second-screen experience" and part of the overarching **"Open Happiness"** brand platform.

---

## 2. Super Bowl / Year

**Super Bowl XLVI**, played **February 5, 2012**, at Lucas Oil Stadium in Indianapolis. New York Giants defeated the New England Patriots. Viewership: **111.3 million** (Nielsen record at the time — the most-watched TV event in US history to that point).

---

## 3. The Mechanic — How It Worked

This was a pioneering **second-screen, live-stream experience** — the first time animated CGI characters had been deployed in real-time for any major media event.

**The concept:** Two animated Coca-Cola polar bears (one a Giants fan in a red scarf, one a Patriots fan in a blue/grey scarf) sat on their "snowfa" (snow sofa) in an Arctic ice cave watching the Super Bowl on TV. Audiences could watch alongside them at **CokePolarBowl.com** and via Facebook.

**How the livestream worked:**
- The bears' reactions were streamed live throughout the entire game — all plays, the halftime show (Madonna), and every commercial break.
- Animators in a **control room at MLB Advanced Media** in New York City "puppeted" the bears in real time using a combination of **Xbox/video game controllers** and a **proprietary PC-based interface** built specifically for this campaign.
- Two W+K creatives (Heather Ryder and her partner) operated the bears live on game day.
- The bears could react with cheers, disgust, laughter, boredom, excitement — responding to actual game events as they happened.
- Guest characters appeared: a penguin, a young polar bear cub. When adult-content commercials aired, a toddler bear would walk past and have its eyes covered by giant paws.

**Social integration:**
- In a brand first, the bears "took over" the **@CocaCola Twitter account** on game day. The profile photo changed to the bears.
- Hashtag: **#GameDayPolarBears**
- Bears would respond to fan tweets in real time; fans could send photos of their own Super Bowl parties to be displayed on the bears' tablet in the live stream.
- W+K copywriters operated the Twitter voice of the bears throughout the game.
- Livestream was also accessible via **rich-media banners on sites like ESPN.com**.
- Note: For technical reasons, the live stream was not extended to tablets and smartphones — those focused on the social elements only.

**TV spots (broadcast on NBC during Super Bowl XLVI):**
- **"Superstition"** — :30 — Q1 — One bear watches the game with all limbs and scarf crossed for luck; his friend helps him drink a Coke through a straw.
- **"Catch"** — :60 — Q2 — Existed in two versions; which ran depended on which team was losing at the time. The bear whose team was behind stepped out of the cave and fumbled a flying bottle of Coke, turning it into inadvertent football moves.
- **"Arghh!"** — :30 — A third spot aired post-game/Q3 as the game's fortunes shifted.
- Both "Catch" and "Arghh!" had multiple pre-produced versions ready to deploy depending on game score.
- Each TV spot ended with a call to action directing viewers to CokePolarBowl.com.

**Post-game:** Fans could send digital Coke coupons via Facebook — "AHHH Giver" for winning-team fans, "ARGH Reliever" for losing-team fans.

**WWF tie-in:** For each RSVP on Facebook before game day, Coca-Cola donated $1 to the World Wildlife Fund for polar bear conservation.

**Billboard campaign:** 41 markets across the US featured outdoor boards inviting people to join the polar bears for the Super Bowl.

## 4. Technical Execution — Framestore & Animal Logic

The technology was described at the time as **"a technological first for the big game or any media event."**

**Framestore** (New York and London offices) and **Animal Logic** (Sydney, Australia) jointly developed the animations and digital technology. The streaming infrastructure was hosted and delivered via **MLB Advanced Media** (MLBAM) — the digital, streaming, and interactive arm of Major League Baseball, which was the industry leader in streaming content at the time.

**How the bears were animated:**
- A library of pre-built CGI animations and reactions was created in advance.
- On game day, animators in the MLB Advanced Media control room in New York used **Xbox-style gaming controllers** plus a proprietary PC-based interface to trigger, combine, and blend these pre-rendered animation sequences in response to live game events.
- This was **not full real-time CGI rendering** (which would have been impossible in 2012 at broadcast quality) — instead it was a sophisticated **real-time puppeteering system** driving a library of pre-rendered animated clips and transitions, assembled live.
- Mike Woods, then Head of Studio and Executive Creative Director at Framestore's Digital and VR department, led the development of this real-time animation workflow. He later described it as one of the pioneering real-time animation projects that shaped his career before he left Framestore in 2017.

**Framestore's role specifically:**
- CGI animation: creating the polar bear characters for the digital stream.
- Development of the real-time animation triggering technology (the "puppeting" system).
- The TV spot animation was handled collaboratively with Animal Logic (Sydney) for the broadcast-quality pre-rendered spots.

## 7. Metrics / Reach

- **9 million total digital streams** (9MM) during and after the game
- **28 minutes average viewing time** — far exceeding the industry benchmark of 10 minutes for live streaming
- **111.3 million** TV viewers for Super Bowl XLVI (NBC)
- **12.2 million social media comments** generated around the Super Bowl overall (Bluefin Labs) — the biggest social TV event in recorded history at that time
- **Coca-Cola ranked #3** brand in social media "share of voice" during the game (11%) behind Doritos (14%) and Budweiser (13%)
- Billboards in **41 markets** ran before and during Super Bowl period

## 10. TV Broadcast vs. Online — Both

The campaign operated simultaneously across:
- **NBC television broadcast** (Super Bowl XLVI): TV spots aired during Q1 ("Superstition"), Q2 ("Catch"), and Q3/post ("Arghh!"). These were broadcast to ~111 million viewers.
- **Online livestream** at CokePolarBowl.com: Bears watching in real-time throughout the game. Accessible via desktop computer and laptop. Also distributed via Facebook.
- **Social media**: @CocaCola Twitter (bears took over), #GameDayPolarBears
- **Rich-media banners** on ESPN.com and other sports sites

The TV spots and the digital livestream were *synchronized* — when ads aired on TV, the bears in the livestream would react to those same ads. The TV spots concluded with a CTA to visit CokePolarBowl.com.

## 11. Key Context and Legacy

- This was the **sixth consecutive year** Coca-Cola advertised during the Super Bowl.
- The campaign drew explicit comparison to W+K's landmark **Old Spice "Responses"** campaign (real-time social interaction with a brand character), which Hal Curtis, Jeff Gillette, and Iain Tait had all been part of at W+K.
- The white Coke cans controversy: just months before the Polar Bowl, in October 2011, Coke had turned its iconic red cans white for a polar bear conservation campaign — but this was discontinued after consumer confusion with Diet Coke cans. The Polar Bowl was seen partly as rehabilitation of the polar bear as a Coke asset.
- Coca-Cola chose **NOT** to repeat the format in 2013 Super Bowl, despite the 9 million streams, citing desire to avoid "the sequel trap." Instead they ran the "Mirage" choose-your-ending campaign.
- In 2026, Pepsi hired Framestore to create CG polar bears for their "The Choice" Super Bowl spot (directed by Taika Waititi) — effectively referencing Framestore's history with the polar bear format established in the Polar Bowl.
- Jeff Gillette (who was "Geoff" in Iain's recollection — his full name is Jeff Gillette) is now an Assistant Teaching Professor of Advertising at the University of Colorado Boulder (CU CMDI), where he co-directs The Brand Studios graduate program.

# Room 10101

**Venue:** Designer's Block, London

A physical/digital installation created by POKE London for Nokia and exhibited at Designer's Block, London, 2004. Visitors sent an SMS to a dedicated Nokia number containing any word of their choice. The system performed a real-time Google Image Search on that word and projected the resulting images onto the walls of the room — turning the space into a dynamically shifting visual environment built entirely from visitors' collective imagination.

Named one of three defining works of Iain's POKE tenure in *Boards Magazine* (2010), alongside Oasis Rubberduckzilla and Orange Unlimited. Won **BIMA 2004, Best Arts and Culture**.

---

## The Experience

1. Visitors entered the room at Designer's Block, London
2. They texted any word to a dedicated Nokia SMS number
3. The system "hacked" Google Image Search — searching in real-time for that word
4. The returned images were projected onto the walls of the room
5. The room was continuously, dynamically repainted by visitors' text submissions

**The name:** "Room 10101" is a binary reference to George Orwell's *Room 101* from *Nineteen Eighty-Four* — the room where the Thought Police confronted each prisoner with their personal worst fear. The binary reframing (10101 = 21 in decimal) both elevates the joke mathematically and inverts the concept entirely: instead of a room containing fears, this was a room decorated by collective imagination and curiosity.

**Nokia's role:** The client provided both the brief and the mobile SMS infrastructure. In 2004, Nokia was the dominant global handset manufacturer, and this project positioned Nokia at the intersection of mobile communication and participatory cultural experience — well before smartphones.

---

## Historical Significance

Room 10101 sits at the intersection of several practices that were just forming in 2004:

**SMS as creative input (2004):** Using SMS to participate in a physical installation was genuinely novel. The iPhone was three years away; SMS was the primary mobile interaction mode.

**"Hacked" API as creative tool:** Google launched its Image Search in 2001 and its developer API in 2002. Using it as a generative, real-time artistic engine was an early example of what would later be called "API art" — software infrastructure co-opted for creative expression.

**Participatory projection installation:** Visitors literally built the room's visual environment in real time. This predates the mainstream projection-mapping and participatory installation wave of the late 2000s by several years.

**Proto-AR framing:** An algorithmically-generated image layer overlaid onto a physical space, triggered by mobile input. A decade before AR became a widespread concept.

Designer's Block as the exhibition venue specifically positioned this as cultural/arts work rather than advertising — confirmed by the BIMA Best Arts and Culture category win.

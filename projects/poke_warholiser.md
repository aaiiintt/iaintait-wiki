# The Warholiser

One of POKE London's first significant digital commissions — a user-participation website built for Tate Modern's *Warhol* retrospective exhibition (7 February – 1 April 2002). Users uploaded a photo of themselves; POKE's software applied a Warhol-style pop art treatment; the resulting portrait was displayed live on the Tate website for exactly 15 minutes.

The 15-minute mechanic was a direct conceptual reference to Andy Warhol's most famous (if mythologised) statement: "In the future, everyone will be world-famous for 15 minutes." The work predated social media photo sharing and user-generated content culture by several years.

---

## The Experience

1. Users visited the Tate website during the exhibition period (February–April 2002)
2. They uploaded a photo of themselves
3. POKE's software applied a Warhol-style colour separation and pop art treatment
4. The resulting portrait appeared live on the Tate site for 15 minutes — a digital "15 minutes of fame"

**Scale (from Nik Roope, Shots Magazine, 2016):** "Thousands uploaded images and hundreds of thousands visited the site."

**Nik Roope's framing (Shots, 2016):** *"It wasn't an ad. It was Warhol's art as a completely scaleable experience that you could participate in, or enjoy as some nice images to look at... That was amazing, and rammed home that there was this new space opening up, an appetite for a new kind of proposition and experience."*

The work was an early proof-of-concept for participatory digital experience — user-generated content, ephemeral display, and direct audience participation with an artist's legacy — ideas that would not become mainstream for another five or six years.

---

## Exhibition Context

**Exhibition:** *Warhol* at Tate Modern, London
**Dates:** 7 February – 1 April 2002
**Sponsor:** UBS Warburg
**Curator:** Donna De Salvo
**Scale:** Over 200 works — described at the time as the largest Warhol retrospective ever staged

The exhibition was a major London cultural event: Channel 4 broadcast a documentary series, and Warhol imagery appeared on billboards across the city. The Warholiser was the digital extension of this campaign.

## Cultural Legacy

The Warholiser is cited in Faris Yakob's *Paid Attention: Innovative Advertising for a Digital World* (Kogan Page, 2015 and 2021 editions) as an early case study in innovative participatory digital advertising. Yakob is a respected industry strategist; the book is used in advertising education. This constitutes canonical industry-textbook status.

In 2016, Nik Roope described the Warholiser as the foundational experience that established POKE's "living ideas" philosophy — the idea that digital work could be an experience rather than an advertisement.
